Garden Journal: Why Writing It Down Triples Your Yield Next Year

By the maker of Henalytics · May 2026 · 5 min read

Try this: without looking it up, name the variety of tomato that did the best in your garden two summers ago. Now name the one that struggled. Now tell me what week you planted them.

If you're like most gardeners, you remember the answer to question one in vague terms ("the cherry one was great"), but the specific variety name is gone. The struggle one you've completely forgotten. The planting date is anyone's guess. Maybe it was Memorial Day. Maybe earlier.

This is how most gardeners spend year after year — repeating the same general activities, occasionally remembering vague impressions, but never accumulating specific knowledge that compounds. The fix is keeping a garden journal. It doesn't have to be elaborate. But it does have to be done.

What a garden journal actually is

A garden journal is any system that lets you answer specific questions about your garden's past. The bare minimum:

  • What did you plant, and when?
  • What did each plant produce (yield, taste, problems)?
  • What was the weather doing?
  • What did each thing cost in materials?

If you can answer those four categories for last year, you can plan this year intelligently. If you can answer them for the last three years, you start having actual gardening intuition that's based on your specific yard and not a magazine.

The five things worth recording (and the five that aren't)

Worth recording

  1. Planting dates and exact varieties. "Cherokee Purple tomato — transplanted April 28." Not "tomatoes — late April." The variety matters because next year you might want it again, and you won't remember the name.
  2. Harvest yields by plant. "Cherokee Purple — 14 lbs total over July to September." This is what tells you which varieties are pulling their weight.
  3. Issues and problems. "Cucumbers got powdery mildew week of July 12 after a heavy rain." Patterns emerge over years that you can't see in one season.
  4. Photos at key moments. Looking at a photo from last June will trigger memories the words won't. Especially helpful for diagnosing issues year-over-year.
  5. Costs of inputs. Seeds, soil, fertilizer, drip lines. Lets you actually know if the garden is paying for itself.

Not worth recording (for most gardeners)

  1. Daily watering frequency. Too granular, you'll abandon the journal trying to keep up.
  2. Soil pH unless you have a specific problem. Most home gardeners don't actually use the data.
  3. Detailed weather you can pull from a weather service later anyway.
  4. Crop rotation diagrams (unless you're getting into serious permaculture).
  5. Inspirational quotes. They feel productive. They aren't.

The compounding case

Here's why this stops feeling like a chore around year two: every entry pays off later.

"The Brandywine I planted on May 5 took until August 28 to produce a single tomato. The same variety planted April 22 the year before was producing by mid-July. Six weeks of difference for the same plant. Next year: April 22 it is."

That's a real insight from real records that you literally cannot have without writing things down. And it shaves six weeks off your fruiting timeline next year.

Multiply that by 20 vegetables in a typical garden, multiplied by the number of years you keep records, and your yields don't just get better — they get systematically better in ways that match your specific conditions.

The three forms of garden journals

1. Paper notebook

Pros: zero friction to start, no batteries, beautiful when filled.
Cons: hard to search, easy to lose, takes effort to flip through years to find a pattern.
Good for: people who already journal in some form, or who want a tactile keepsake.

2. Spreadsheet

Pros: searchable, sortable, can chart yields over time.
Cons: tedious to add to from your phone in the garden, gets messy fast.
Good for: spreadsheet people who have an existing data habit.

3. Garden tracking app

Pros: built for the use case, captures photos and weather automatically, syncs across devices, surfaces year-over-year comparisons without effort.
Cons: depends on the app being good (many aren't).
Good for: people who want it to be effortless.

The "you don't have to be perfect" rule

Most gardeners who try journaling abandon it because they miss a few days and feel like the record is "ruined." It isn't. A garden journal with 60% of your activity logged is infinitely more useful than no journal at all. The goal is not completeness. The goal is enough.

Skip a week. Skip a month. Come back when you remember. The pattern that emerges over years is robust to gaps.
