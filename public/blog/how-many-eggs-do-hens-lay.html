How Many Eggs Do Backyard Hens Actually Lay Per Year?

By the maker of Henalytics · May 2026 · 5 min read

Hatchery websites will tell you their hens lay 280-300 eggs per year. New chicken-keepers do the math: "Six hens × 280 eggs = 1,680 eggs per year. That's almost 5 eggs per day!" Then they get their hens, count the eggs over a year, and end up with maybe 1,200. What gives?

The hatchery numbers are correct under hatchery conditions: a young hen, in her first laying year, fed industrial layer mash, with artificial 14-hour daylight, in a climate-controlled barn. Your backyard hen is none of those things. Here's what to actually expect.

Eggs by hen age

The single biggest factor in egg production is hen age. Production peaks in year 1 and declines steadily after.

AgeTypical eggs per year
Year 1 (after starting to lay around month 5)250 – 290
Year 2200 – 240
Year 3150 – 200
Year 4100 – 150
Year 5+50 – 100, decreasing

This is why commercial egg operations cull their flocks at 18 months. Backyard keepers don't, because we tend to keep our hens around for the personality. But it does mean a 6-bird mixed-age flock won't produce the same as 6 first-year birds.

Eggs by month (the seasonal curve)

Hens are highly photoperiodic — they need 12+ hours of daylight to keep laying consistently. Without supplemental lighting, expect this rough pattern in the northern hemisphere:

MonthEggs per hen (typical)
January5 – 12
February10 – 18
March18 – 25
April22 – 28
May23 – 28
June22 – 27
July20 – 25
August18 – 22
September10 – 18 (molting begins for some hens)
October5 – 15 (molting + shorter days)
November2 – 10
December2 – 8

The summer peak is real. Some weeks in May or June you might genuinely get an egg per hen per day. The winter slump is also real — many backyard keepers go 2-3 months with very few eggs in December and January.

Why your hens stopped laying (the troubleshooting list)

If your hens were laying well and suddenly stopped, the cause is almost always one of these:

  • Molting (most common) — happens once a year, usually in fall. Hens drop feathers and re-grow them. Laying stops or drops dramatically for 4-8 weeks. Nothing to fix; they'll come back.
  • Shorter days — fewer than 12 hours of daylight signals "winter" to a hen's body. Production drops naturally Oct-Feb.
  • Stress — predator scares, dog visits, moving the coop, new flockmates, even loud weather. Stress shuts off laying for days to weeks.
  • Broodiness — a hen has decided to sit on eggs and hatch them. She'll stop laying for 21+ days.
  • Heat — temperatures over 90°F slow production.
  • Diet imbalance — too many treats, scratch, or kitchen scraps means too little protein/calcium. Get them back to layer feed as 90% of their diet.
  • Age — if your hens are 4+ years old and production is dropping, that's just how it goes.
  • Hidden eggs — sometimes hens find a secret laying spot somewhere else in the yard. Check under shrubs and in corners.
  • Illness — less common, but worth ruling out if symptoms are sudden and dramatic.
